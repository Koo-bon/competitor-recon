# Market-Research Frameworks (the professional toolkit)

These are the standard frameworks a professional market researcher / strategy
consultant runs. Each entry: **what it is → why it matters → how to fill it →
what "good" looks like.** Apply the ones the interview goal calls for; don't
force all of them. Table of contents:

1. Market framing: TAM / SAM / SOM
2. Demand framing: Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD)
3. Market attractiveness: Porter's Five Forces
4. Strategy core: STP (Segmentation → Targeting → Positioning)
5. Offer teardown: 4P / 7P marketing mix
6. Positioning map (perceptual map)
7. Synthesis: SWOT + the "so-what"

---

## 1. TAM / SAM / SOM — how big is this really?
- **What:** Total Addressable Market → Serviceable Available Market → Serviceable Obtainable Market. Three shrinking circles of opportunity.
- **Why:** Answers "is this worth entering?" Grounds every later claim in a real number.
- **How:** Find category size (industry reports, Statista, press). TAM = whole category revenue. SAM = the slice matching the user's segment + geography. SOM = realistic share in 1–3 yrs given competition. Always cite the source and mark ✅/🟡/⚠️.
- **Good looks like:** three numbers with sources, and one sentence on what share is realistically winnable.

## 2. Jobs-To-Be-Done — what is the customer really hiring this for?
- **What:** The functional + emotional + social "job" the product is hired to do. ("I don't buy a keyring; I hire it to signal my taste and feel a small daily joy.")
- **Why:** Selling points only make sense against the job. Two brands can sell the same object for totally different jobs.
- **How:** For the category, write the job as: *"When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."* Then note functional vs. emotional vs. social dimensions. Identify which job each competitor serves.
- **Good looks like:** 1–3 clearly worded jobs, each mapped to which competitor owns it.

## 3. Porter's Five Forces — is this market attractive or brutal?
- **What:** Five pressures that decide profitability: (1) competitive rivalry, (2) threat of new entrants, (3) supplier power, (4) buyer power, (5) threat of substitutes.
- **Why:** Tells the user how hard the fight is *before* they commit money.
- **How:** Rate each force Low / Medium / High with a one-line reason grounded in the collected data. Summarize: attractive, tough, or brutal.
- **Good looks like:** a 5-row rating table + a verdict sentence.

## 4. STP — the spine of the whole strategy
- **Segmentation:** break the market into distinct buyer groups (by need, demographic, behavior, price sensitivity).
- **Targeting:** which segment(s) are underserved / winnable — the gap.
- **Positioning:** the one sentence claim that makes the brand the obvious choice for that segment. Use the classic template: *"For [target] who [need], [brand] is the [category] that [key benefit], unlike [competitor], because [reason to believe]."*
- **Why:** This is where research becomes strategy. Every professional deck lands here.
- **Good looks like:** named segments, one chosen target with rationale, one crisp positioning statement.

## 5. 4P / 7P mix — how each competitor actually operates
- **4P:** Product, Price, Place (channel), Promotion. **7P adds:** People, Process, Physical evidence (for retail/experience brands).
- **Why:** Forces a complete, comparable teardown instead of cherry-picking.
- **How:** One row per competitor per P. This feeds the comparison matrix.
- **Good looks like:** a filled matrix where every cell is sourced.

## 6. Positioning / perceptual map
- **What:** Plot competitors on the 2 axes that matter most for THIS user's decision (from the interview). Common axes: price (low↔high), IP-based↔original, mass↔niche, functional↔emotional.
- **Why:** The single most legible way to reveal white space at a glance.
- **How:** ASCII/quadrant map. Cluster crowded corners; the empty quadrant is the opportunity.
- **Good looks like:** one map where the gap is visually obvious.

## 7. SWOT + "so-what"
- **What:** Strengths / Weaknesses (internal to the user's planned venture) vs. Opportunities / Threats (external, from the research).
- **Why:** Converts all the analysis into the user's own action list.
- **How:** 4 quadrants, 2–4 bullets each, every bullet traceable to a finding. End with 3 concrete "so-what" moves.
- **Good looks like:** no generic bullets — each one references specific evidence gathered.
